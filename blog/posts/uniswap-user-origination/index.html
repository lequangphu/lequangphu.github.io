Introduction

If you see error of loading charts, please refresh to reload this page (a few times if necessary).

For general tracking and updated data, please visit this dashboard.

A Defi user (trader) trades in Uniswap pools by either (1) swapping through graphic user interface, i.e. on a DEX website, or (2) directly calling the swap function of a smart contract through API. The former is for normal, every day users, and the latter is for advance users who know smart contract programming language such as Solidity. In either ways, a smart contract is called.

Since launched in early November 2018, Uniswap saw 450K+ contracts called by 11.3M traders to trade $1.6T volume in 276M+ number of trades in 241K+ pools.

The contracts can be grouped into a few routes by referring to contracts’ label supported by popular crypto application such as a block explorer, or a crypto analytic platform. In this post, I use label provided by Dune (the main source), and Etherscan to group the contracts into routes. The routes include (1) Uniswap Frontend, (2) Aggregators, (3) MEV, (4) Oku, and (5) Unknowns. What are these routes, exactly?

Uniswap Frontend is Uniswap official application, where normal traders use to execute their trades.

Aggregators is other DEXs’ applications, where normal traders use to execute their trades.

MEV is smart contracts, usually called bots, where advance traders use to execute their trades to exploit MEV.

Oku is an advanced application, backed by Uniswap Foundation, launched in early June 2023, where normal traders can execute market order or limit order. Caveat: a market order from Oku is indistinguishable from a swap on Uniswap Frontend, thus is included in Uniswap Frontend route.

Unknowns is a group of smart contracts, which are not yet labeled.

Number of contract of the routes range between 1 (Oku) and 349K (Aggregators).

Caveat: Uniswap Frontend has a few main contracts called router. Most of 1.8K contracts you see in the chart below are Uniswap v1 pools (exchanges in v1 technical term). In Uniswap v1, pools contract are called directly, which makes it hard to differentiate between (1) trade made on Uniswap application and (2) trade made through API. My assumption here is the v1 pools contracts were mainly called by the type (1) trade. Thus I include all of the contracts in Uniswap Frontend.

For every 100 contracts called, 79 in Aggregators, 20 in Unknowns, MEV and Uniswap Frontend roughly equally split the rest.

We have learned the definition about the trading routes, which a trader can take to trade in Uniswap pools. The rest of this post, let’s explore the routes’ role in onboarding trader and growing Uniswap trades.

If not specified, data is of all time Uniswap history until July 30, 2023.

Key Takeaways

  1. Uniswap Frontend onboarded the most trader to Uniswap, MEV contributes the most to total Uniswap volume.

  2. About a third of total traders, onboarded by each route, are multi-route traders. Of multi-route traders, two third became multi-route in the first nine weeks, a third became multi-route after week ninth. More than half of multi-route traders, who trade on Uniswap Frontend, trade more through Uniswap Frontend than on the other routes.

  3. Traders who started Uniswap Frontend or MEV tend to stick longer to their first route than those started with the other routes.

  4. Return traders mainly drive the routes’ growth, and it is more important to MEV’s growth than the other routes. In volume, Uniswap Frontend has the fastest weekly growth rate. In number of new trader onboarded to Uniswap, Unknowns has the fastest weekly growth rate.

  5. Traders of Uniswap Frontend and MEV have both trade size and trade frequency larger than those of traders the other routes.

  6. A third of total Oku traders, trade more on Oku than on the other routes. On average, traders pay less gas fee on Oku than on the other routes.

3 big questions

Which route onboard the most number of trader to Uniswap? What are the routes’ contribution to total Uniswap volume?

A trader is counted to a route if the route onboard the trader to Uniswap, i.e. the trader executed the first Uniswap trade on the route.

Number of trader onboarded by the routes range between 2 (Oku) and 6.3M (Uniswap Frontend).

For every 100 traders, 55 started with Uniswap Frontend, 35 started with Aggregators, 9 started with Unknowns, and 1 started with MEV.

Volume of the routes range between $97K+ (Oku) and $678B+ (MEV).

For every $100 in total volume, $42 from MEV, $29 from Uniswap Frontend, $15 from Aggregators, and $14 from Unknowns.

Do traders trade in multiple routes?

All the routes have traders, who trade in multiple routes.

For every 100 traders started with Uniswap Frontend, 26 traders trade in multiple routes.

For every 100 traders started with Aggregators, 23 traders trade in multiple routes.

For every 100 traders started with MEV, 38 traders trade in multiple routes.

For every 100 traders started with Unknowns, 26 traders trade in multiple routes.

All traders, who started with Oku, trade in multiple routes.

Number of week after the first trade that a trader trade in the second route

Of the routes other than Oku, traders who trade in multiple routes, which average ages range between 21 weeks (Unknowns) and 40 weeks (Uniswap Frontend), are much older than the traders who don’t, which average ages range between 3 weeks (MEV) and 8 weeks (Uniswap Frontend).

For every 100 traders who start trading in their second route, 32 traders start in the first week (week 0), 31 traders start between the second week (week 1) and the ninth week (week 8), 37 traders start after week 8.

For every 100 traders who started with Uniswap Frontend, then start trading in their second route, 29 traders start in week 0, 28 traders start between week 1 and week 8, 43 traders start after week 8.

For every 100 traders who started with Aggregators, then start trading in their second route, 34 traders start in week 0, 36 traders start between week 1 and week 8, 30 traders start after week 8.

For every 100 traders who started with MEV, then start trading in their second route, 29 traders start in week 0, 31 traders start between week 1 and week 8, 40 traders start after week 8.

For every 100 traders who started with Unknowns, then start trading in their second route, 42 traders start in week 0, 33 traders start between week 1 and week 8, 25 traders start after week 8.

Of 2 traders who started with Oku, then start trading in their second route, 1 traders start in week 0, 1 traders start between week 1 and week 8.

We learned that

  1. Uniswap Frontend onboarded the most trader to Uniswap, account for 55 traders in every 100 new traders of Uniswap.

  2. MEV contributes the most to total Uniswap volume, account for $42 in every $100 of total Uniswap volume.

  3. About a third of total traders, onboarded by each route, are multi-route traders.

  4. Of multi-route traders, two third became multi-route in the first nine weeks, a third became multi-route after week ninth.

  5. Traders who started Uniswap Frontend or MEV tend to become multi-route traders later than those started with the other routes. In other words, traders who started Uniswap Frontend or MEV tend to stick longer to their first route than those started with the other routes.

Growth of the routes

I measure growth of a route with weekly growth rate, in volume and in number of trader onboarded by the route. My calculation is based on exponential growth formula.

The formula is \(C = I(1 + G)^{T}\), where \(C\), the current value, is total value of [volume, trader] the route, \(I\), the initial value, is value of the first stable week of the route, i.e. the first week that the route saw trading activity happened on all 7 days, \(T\) is number of weeks between \(I\) and \(C\).

Weekly volume growth rate of the routes range between 6.6% (Aggregators and MEV) and 7.4% (Uniswap Frontend).

Weekly trader growth rate of the routes range between 5% (MEV) and 5.8% (Unknowns).

Volume of the routes have grown faster than number of new traders, which the routes onboarded to Uniswap.

What type of trader, new or return, drive the growth of the routes?

Traders, who are new to a route, not necessarily new to Uniswap, are the new traders of the route.

On average, every week, for every 100 traders, the new traders of the routes range between 13 (MEV) and 43 (Aggregators and Unknowns). This implies return traders play a more important role in driving the routes growth.

An other point is despite similar growth rate in volume and number of trader, share of new trader of MEV is only a third of those of other routes.

In case you are not familiar with retention rate, you can read more about it here.

In the first 106 weeks (i.e. the first 2 years), with the first week is week 0:

  • In week 1, retention rate of MEV drop to 48%, while of other routes drop to between 15% and 23%. After that, retention rate of MEV usually is at least double those of other routes. This offsets the difference in new trader share we learned above, which affects the routes growth.

  • Trailing behind MEV is Uniswap Frontend, which retention rate flat above 4%.

  • After week 100, Aggregators retention rate drop to as low as 2%, and Unknowns rentention rate drop to sub 1%.

We learned that

  1. In volume, Uniswap Frontend has the fastest weekly growth rate, while Aggregators and MEV have the slowest weekly growth rate.

  2. In number of new trader onboarded to Uniswap, Unknowns has the fastest weekly growth rate, while MEV has the slowest weekly growth rate.

  3. Return traders mainly drive the routes’ growth, and it is more important to MEV’s growth than the other routes.

Trader behavior of the routes

A trader behaviors include (1) volume per week (volume), and (2) number of trade per week (trade frequency). Comparing median values shows the difference between normal traders of the routes. And comparing average values shows more of the difference between large traders of the routes.

Median volume per trader of the routes range between $38 (Oku) and $407 (MEV).

Average volume per trader of the routes range between $3K (Oku) and $210K (MEV).

Median trade frequency (number of trade per week) per trader of the routes range between 1 (Oku) and 1.6 (Uniswap Frontend).

Average trade frequency per trader of the routes range between 1.9 (Oku) and 8.2 (Uniswap Frontend).

As large values cause the difference between average and median, when we divide average by median, we can compare the weight of large values (large volume or high frequency traders) between the routes.

Average/Median volume of the routes range between 21 (Uniswap Frontend) and 516 (MEV).

Compare to volume, the difference of the weight of large values (high frequency traders) between the routes are much less in trade frequency. Average/median trade frequency of the routes range between 2 (Uniswap Frontend, Aggregators, and Oku) and 8 (MEV).

We learned that

  1. Volume per week of normal traders of Uniswap Frontend and MEV are quite similar to each other, and much higher than those of the other 3 routes.

  2. Volume per week of large traders of Uniswap Frontend and MEV is at least 20 folds higher than those of the other 4 routes.

  3. Number of trade per week of normal traders of Uniswap Frontend is at least a half higher than those of the other 4 routes.

  4. Number of trade per week of large traders of MEV is at least double those of the other 4 routes.

Oku

With limit order, there are 3 actions a trader can do on Oku advance trading interface: place New order, Cancel a not-yet-filled order, and Claim a filled order.

  • A trader starts by placing a limit order, which calls the function newOrder of the LimitOrderRegistry contract deployed by Oku, which returns the NewOrder event.

  • If the order is not filled, the trader can cancel the order, which calls the newOrder function, which returns the CancelOrder event.

  • If the order is filled, which returns the OrderFilled event, then the trader can claim the order, which calls the claimOrder function, which returns the ClaimOrder event.

If you are familiar with developing smart contract on Ethereum, you can read more about the contract code here. The diagram below roughly demonstrates the whole process.

For every $100 in Oku volume, $97 in Arbitrum, $2 in Ethereum, and $1 in Optimism.

32 traders placed $97K+ volume (NewOrder), 18 of 32 traders canceled $14K+, 14 of 32 traders claimed $3.6K out of $4.4K of total volume of filled orders, which means only 4.5% of total volume has been filled.

Of 32 traders, Oku only onboarded 2 traders to Uniswap.

Of 32 traders of Oku, 10 traders trade more on Oku, in average volume per trade, than on the other routes.

Do traders pay less gas fee on Oku than on the other routes?

Average Gas Fee per Trade of the routes range between $2.5 (Oku) and $45.7 (MEV).

Other than MEV, gas fee of Uniswap Frontend is higher than the other routes. Oku gas fee is only an eighth of that of Uniswap Frontend.

Yes, traders pay less gas fee on Oku than on the other routes.
